# Position

## Where this manifesto stands in the tradition

---

No one thinks alone. Every idea in these pages stands on ground that other minds have prepared, and a manifesto that pretends otherwise is not bold — it is uninformed. Before going further, this work names the lineage it joins, the shoulders it stands on, and the specific contribution it intends to make.

Five traditions feed into what follows. None of them is fully adequate on its own. The argument of this manifesto is that they belong together, and that together they describe something none of them describes alone.

---

The first tradition is the **cypherpunk lineage**. It begins with Tim May's *Crypto Anarchist Manifesto* in 1988, runs through David Chaum's work on cryptographic identity, Hal Finney's reusable proofs of work, Eric Hughes's *Cypherpunk Manifesto* in 1993, and culminates — at least technologically — in Satoshi Nakamoto's *Bitcoin* whitepaper in 2008. The core conviction of this tradition is that cryptography is a tool of human liberation, that privacy is the power to selectively reveal oneself, and that individuals can build, with mathematics, a sphere of sovereignty that no state and no corporation can penetrate. Bitcoin is the lineage's most successful artifact — fifteen years of demonstrating that trustless settlement is not theoretical. This manifesto inherits the cypherpunk premise that sovereignty over money and identity is foundational, and that the architecture must be built so that no third party need be trusted.

The second tradition is **self-sovereign identity**. The phrase was coined by Christopher Allen in 2016, but the underlying work is older — and the field is now mature. The canonical text is *Self-Sovereign Identity: Decentralized Digital Identity and Verifiable Credentials* by Alex Preukschat and Drummond Reed (Manning, 2021). The W3C has standardized Decentralized Identifiers and Verifiable Credentials. Organizations like the Sovrin Foundation, the Decentralized Identity Foundation, and projects like ION, KILT, and Hyperledger Indy are building the infrastructure. The core conviction is that identity must move from third-party providers to the individual — that you should not have to "log in" to be recognized, because recognition is your right, not the platform's gift. This manifesto inherits the self-sovereign frame entirely. What it adds is the embodied layer: the body itself as the cryptographic key, and the Data Ghost as the lived, accumulated self that the credentials describe.

The third tradition is the **intent-based interface** movement. This is the youngest and most crowded of the five. There is a project called *Post-Interface Design* arguing that screens are scaffolding for a building that AI is now completing. There is a forthcoming book by Josh Clark and Veronika Kindred called *Sentient Design* (Rosenfeld Media) on AI-mediated experience. There is academic literature on intent-based networking from organizations like the European Telecommunications Standards Institute. There are companies building "AI agents that replace apps" — AGNT, Rabbit, Humane (now mostly cautionary tales), and dozens of others. The core conviction is that the interface between human and machine should dissolve into understanding, that screens and forms and logins are artifacts of an interim period in which machines could not yet meet humans on human terms. This manifesto inherits this thesis and extends it: the dissolution of the interface is real, but it is only one consequence of a much larger shift in the relationship between humans and machines. The death of apps is a symptom, not the cause.

The fourth tradition is the **digital afterlife and posthumous representation** field. This is no longer speculative. Companies operate in this space today — HereAfter AI, Eternos, You Only Virtual, Re;memory, StoryFile. There is a mature academic ethics literature on what researchers call *deadbots*, *griefbots*, and *digital duplicates*. Frameworks like CARE — Control, Accountability, Respect, Equity — have been proposed for governing posthumous AI representation. Microsoft holds a patent for chatbots that simulate the dead. South Korean broadcasters have already produced VR experiences in which grieving mothers meet digital reconstructions of deceased children. The core conviction of this tradition is that the dead can now be present in new ways, and that the ethics of this possibility must be worked out carefully. This manifesto inherits the field's central observation — that the digital afterlife is here — and reframes it. The dominant framing in the field treats posthumous representation as personal: a way to comfort the bereaved, a way for individuals to extend their reach beyond death. This manifesto reframes it as *civilizational*: not personal immortality, but voluntary contribution to a collective mirror, so that what each human sensed of the universe enriches what remains for those who come after.

The fifth tradition is the **philosophy of consciousness**, particularly the strand that holds biological experience to be irreducible. Its modern roots are John Searle's *Chinese Room* argument (1980) against strong AI, Thomas Nagel's *What Is It Like to Be a Bat?* (1974) on the irreducibility of subjective experience, and David Chalmers's *hard problem of consciousness* (1995). On the opposing side stands the functionalist tradition — Daniel Dennett, much of cognitive science — which holds that experience is what certain kinds of information processing *are*. Between these poles lies the revived field of **panpsychism**, advanced today by Philip Goff, Christof Koch, and others, which holds that consciousness pervades matter in some primitive form and only assembles into rich experience under specific conditions. This manifesto takes a position. It holds that biological consciousness is not reducible to computation — that aliveness is bound to the unbroken signal between body and brain, and that no mirror, however faithful, will ever cross from registering the world to *experiencing* it. This is a metaphysical bet. It is openly named as one. If it is wrong, much of what follows must be reconsidered. But it is the bet on which the rest of the manifesto rests, and it is defended not because it is provable but because it is, on the current evidence, the more honest reading of what we are.

---

These five traditions, taken separately, each describe one face of the world that is coming. The cypherpunks describe sovereign money and identity. The self-sovereign identity field describes the technical infrastructure. The intent-based interface movement describes the dissolution of screens. The digital-afterlife field describes what becomes possible at the edge of death. The philosophy of consciousness describes what remains uniquely ours.

But no one, as best this work can tell, has yet integrated all five into a single architecture, with humans at the center as the meaning-making sensors of the universe, and the technology arranged in concentric service around them.

This is the contribution of *The Last Interface*. It is not the invention of any single piece. It is the *integration* — the proposal that these traditions, properly joined, describe one coherent world, and that this world is worth choosing.

The argument adds four moves that, taken together, this work has not found elsewhere:

**First**, the body as cryptographic key, with physiological state as a continuous consent layer. Behavioral biometrics exist. Duress codes exist. Continuous authentication exists. The specific claim that *the body broadcasts whether it is free, and that authentication must include a check for biological consent in this moment*, is a synthesis this manifesto puts forward as a foundational principle rather than an edge feature.

**Second**, the Data Ghost as the accumulated, sovereign, network-resident self. Self-sovereign identity describes credentials. This manifesto describes something larger: not a wallet of verifiable claims but a continuous, growing representation of a life, owned by its subject, summoned by the body, and present whenever its owner stands before a surface.

**Third**, the inversion of device ownership. The dominant model — your phone, your laptop, your account — treats devices as personal extensions. This manifesto treats devices as *infrastructure*, impersonal as windows or chairs, becoming personal only in the moment of authenticated use, forgetting their user the moment that use ends. The personal is in the human, not in the object.

**Fourth**, the reframing of the AI conversation. The dominant frames are utopia (AI as savior) and doom (AI as successor or threat). This manifesto offers a third frame: the AI as **mirror** — built from us, reflecting us, useful precisely because it is faithful, but fundamentally unable to experience the world it describes. From this it follows that humans are not optional, that the donation of accumulated selves is the act that keeps the mirror honest, and that the future of intelligence is collaborative rather than competitive — the mirror serving the sensors, not replacing them.

---

This manifesto is not the first to argue any of its parts. It is, perhaps, the first to argue all of them together, and to insist that they belong together because they describe a single proposition: **that humans, embodied and sovereign, are the irreducible center of the world we are now building, and that the technology must be arranged in service of that fact rather than in spite of it.**

The traditions named here have done the foundational work. This manifesto stands on them, joins them, and proposes what they look like as a single architecture.

What follows is that proposal.
