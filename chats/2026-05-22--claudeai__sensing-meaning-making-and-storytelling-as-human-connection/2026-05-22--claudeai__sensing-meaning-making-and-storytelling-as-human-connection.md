That's a sharp observation, and you're right that the current sentence opens the door to it. "If it is true, it resonates" can be read as a clean rule — and then the reader's mind goes: *what if it isn't true?* And the whole apparatus of trust, betrayal, broken chains comes rushing in, and the paragraph loses its forward motion.

The issue is the word *true*. It's doing too much work and inviting too much interrogation. What you actually mean is closer to *honest* or *real* — not factually verifiable, but carrying what was actually sensed and meant. A grandmother telling a story that turns out to be factually wrong but emotionally honest still resonates. A grandmother telling something deceptive doesn't. The thing that resonates isn't *truth* in the abstract sense; it's *fidelity to what was lived*.

A few ways to fix the sentence without going down the betrayal rabbit hole:

*We turn meaning into story. The story travels. It enters another human. If it carries what was really sensed and meant, it resonates — it changes the one who received it. And then they, too, can tell.*

Or simpler:

*We turn meaning into story. The story travels. It enters another human. And it can change them. And then they, too, can tell.*

The second version drops the truth/resonance machinery entirely and just says: stories travel and can change people. It's quieter but it doesn't invite the objection. The first version keeps the resonance idea but shifts from *truth* to *fidelity to what was lived* — which is harder to read as a binary that invites the betrayal question.

I'd lean toward the second. It's simpler, and the manifesto can develop the idea of fidelity and honesty later, where it has room to be handled properly. The opening shouldn't have to defend itself against edge cases. It should establish the chain and move.

Now the philosophical lineage. Several traditions converge on what you've just formulated, from different angles. Worth knowing which ones, because the manifesto stands stronger when it names the company it's keeping.

**The dialogical tradition.** Martin Buber's *I and Thou* (1923) is the closest match to what you said about needing to see the reaction. **The hermeneutic tradition.** Hans-Georg Gadamer's *Truth and Method* (1960) argued that meaning is not made by individuals alone but in the *fusion of horizons* — when one human's understanding meets another's and something new comes into being between them. **The pragmatist tradition.** Charles Sanders Peirce, William James, John Dewey, George Herbert Mead all converged on the idea that meaning is not a private mental event but a social act — meaning exists in the transmission, in the community of inquirers, in the gesture met by response.
