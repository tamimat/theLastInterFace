## What happened

Tamim opened with a one-sentence question: *I heard the phrase poetic dishonesty. Who said it and where and why?*

Claude responded that the phrase did not ring an immediate bell and ran three web searches in sequence, each refining the angle:

1. `"poetic dishonesty" phrase origin who said` — returned generic dishonesty-quote pages, nothing on the specific phrase
2. `"poetic dishonesty" essay criticism` — returned Pope's *Essay on Criticism* and unrelated material
3. `"poetic dishonesty" Orwell OR Auden OR Eliot OR Heaney` — returned hits on Auden including *What Auden Gave Up* (Slate) and *Auden in the 21st Century* (Georgia Review)

The third search produced the answer Claude gave: the phrase is not itself a canonical term tied to a single critic, but it closely matches W.H. Auden's repudiation of his own poem *September 1, 1939* as *the most dishonest poem I have ever written* (1967 letter). The trouble was the line *We must love one another or die*, which Auden eventually called false on its face (people die regardless of whether they love each other). Auden first changed *or* to *and*, then suppressed the poem entirely.

Claude noted that critics have since used *poetic dishonesty* loosely about that episode and about the broader question of whether poetry can dress false sentiment in beautiful language. Auden's own earlier framing of the 1930s as *a low dishonest decade*, in the same poem, makes the later self-criticism doubly ironic.
